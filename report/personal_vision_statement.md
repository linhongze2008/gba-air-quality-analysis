# Personal Vision Statement
**(For JS2950 Application - Hong Kong Baptist University)**

**Applicant:** Jayden Lin
**Word Count:** ~600 words

My passion for the natural sciences—specifically the intersection of chemistry, physics, and mathematics—has always been driven by a desire to understand the mechanics of the world around me. However, it was only recently that I realized understanding the world is not enough; we must also use that understanding to protect it. Growing up in Shenzhen, a rapidly developing metropolis in the Greater Bay Area, I have witnessed firsthand the tension between economic expansion and environmental preservation. This observation became the catalyst for my recent independent research project on local air quality and the primary reason I am drawn to the Personalized Pathway at Hong Kong Baptist University.

The traditional boundaries between academic disciplines often feel restrictive when confronting modern global crises. For instance, in my A-Level Chemistry classes, I learned the theoretical mechanisms of atmospheric pollutants, such as the photochemical reactions that produce ground-level ozone. Yet, to truly understand how these pollutants affect my city, I had to step outside the chemistry laboratory. I taught myself Python programming and statistical modeling to analyze six years of daily air quality data from the Shenzhen Ecological Environment Bureau. By integrating mathematical linear regression with meteorological data, I discovered a nuanced reality: while primary particulate matter (PM2.5) in Shenzhen has decreased significantly due to municipal policies, secondary pollutants like ozone are silently rising due to complex atmospheric interactions. 

This project was a revelation. It taught me that solving real-world environmental issues—arguably the most pressing global challenge of our generation—requires more than just chemical knowledge. It requires data science to identify invisible trends, physics to model environmental systems, and an understanding of policy to implement change. This is exactly why I wish to pursue the Personalized Pathway (JS2950). I expect this unique program to allow me to transcend rigid departmental silos. I aim to curate a curriculum that blends environmental science, data analytics, and sustainability studies. The flexibility to design my own academic trajectory is not just appealing; it is essential for the interdisciplinary approach I intend to take.

Looking forward, my short-term goal during my undergraduate studies is to deepen my technical proficiency in machine learning and computational modeling while building a robust foundation in environmental chemistry. I want to transition from analyzing historical data to building real-time, predictive models for urban pollution that can account for complex, non-linear atmospheric dynamics. In the long term, I aspire to become an environmental data scientist or a sustainability consultant, working within the Greater Bay Area to develop data-driven strategies for urban climate resilience. 

Through the Personalized Pathway, I hope to cultivate adaptability, critical thinking, and the ability to synthesize disparate streams of information. I want to develop the resilience to tackle unstructured problems—much like my air quality project, where data was messy and initial hypotheses were often wrong. I am confident that HKBU's commitment to whole-person education, combined with the autonomy of the JS2950 program, will equip me with the cross-disciplinary expertise and the innovative mindset necessary to contribute meaningfully to our global environmental future.
